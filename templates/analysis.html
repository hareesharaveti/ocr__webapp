Strengths and Weaknesses of OCR Families

Traditional OCR (e.g., Tesseract)

Traditional OCR engines are lightweight, easy to deploy on CPU-only environments, and work well on clean printed text for many languages. [web:37][web:57]

They provide limited understanding of document semantics and complex layouts, and typically perform poorly on noisy or handwritten documents without extensive preprocessing. [web:35][web:37][web:73]

Deep Learning OCR (PaddleOCR, EasyOCR, Surya/MMOCR)

Deep learning OCR systems better handle scene text, diverse fonts, distortions, and multilingual content than classical engines. [web:40][web:52][web:98]

These models usually require larger dependencies, occasional GPU acceleration, and more complex configuration, increasing deployment complexity compared with traditional OCR. [web:40][web:52][web:98]

Cloud OCR APIs (Google Vision, Azure, Textract)

Managed cloud OCR APIs deliver strong accuracy, automatic model updates, and built-in support for handwriting and structured extraction, which make them attractive for enterprise pipelines. [web:35][web:40]

The trade-offs include per-page cost, data residency and privacy concerns, and vendor lock-in for large volumes of sensitive documents. [web:35][web:92]

VLM / VLLM-Based Models

Vision-Language Models extend beyond classic OCR by jointly modeling visual content, text tokens, and spatial layout, enabling end-to-end reading, question answering, and summarization over documents. [web:35][web:36][web:93]

Their main limitations are high computational requirements, inference latency, and more complex deployment when compared to lightweight OCR engines or simple cloud APIs. [web:35][web:36][web:41][web:91]
